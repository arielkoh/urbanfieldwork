Urban Fieldworks

Urban Fieldworks is an exercise that investigates the ordinary happenings and encounters found within an urban landscape.

Constellations From Singapore

Our group is fascinated with stars in the night sky, and we have decided to plunge into astronomy in Singapore. While most city-dwellers have the common perception that Singapore is a heavily light-polluted and starless Garden City, we want to explore if that is really the case. Through the pictorial data we collect, we want to explore if there are places in Singapore where we can see the stars in all its magnificent glory.

How our perspective of the stars changes as the Earth rotates will also be explored through the pictures we collect. The quantitative data will be gathered from a variety of places in Singapore—from the bustling heart of town in Orchard, to the edges of Singapore in Pasir Ris, to humbling residential areas such as Bishan or Dover (and many other areas), where we will take photos of the night sky. Which part of Singapore is least light-polluted where one can go and fully appreciate the stars?

Applications that were used for collecting data on constellations: Astrometry, Clear Outside and Night Sky.

Field Trip

After collecting all our data, we sketched out a map of Singapore to visually pinpoint the number of constellations we saw at the varying locations.

We used colours to signify the numbers, and labelled the locations accordingly. It is quite evident that we mainly explored the Southern-Eastern parts of Singapore, since none of us in the group lived far North or West.

Artefact

Our artefact consists of coloured filters and an illustrated background of the constellations in Singapore to present the different constellations visible at the different parts of Singapore. We bought plastic filters of red, blue and green from Art Friend, and coloured the illustration in respective CMYK colours to successfully block certain constellations out when a coloured filter was placed in front of it. The three filter colours represent the perspective from Singapore–for example, the red filter represents perspective from the central part of Singapore where only 5 constellations can be seen, thus being brought out by the filter while the other constellations are blocked out.

Central (RED)

1. Libra 2. Sagittarius 3. Cygnus
4. Corona Australis 5. Draco

West and North (GREEN)

1. Aquila 2. Delphinus 3. Grus 4. Pisces Austrinus
5. Pisces 6. Sagitta 7. Vulpecula

East and Northeast (BLUE)

1. Indus 2. Scutum 3. Aquarius 4. Capricornus
5. Ophiuchus 6. Pegasus 7. Scorpius 8. Tuscana
9. Hercules 10. Bootes

We really struggled with the colour theory aspect of this artefact. Initially based on our reference and inspiration, we thought that each of the filters had to fully block out the rest of the CMYK colours. In that sense, only our red filter worked perfectly. It was after a lot of research and experimentation that the filters did not fully cancel the colours out, but drastically brought certain colours out more than others.

Reflection

As we look back at the process of this field work, from start to finish, we were glad we championed through! We managed to get out and explore around Singapore to collect the data, and our final artefact was also interactive and satisfying to complete. Our group was able to utilise and work around our individual strengths and weaknesses–we even made use of our physical locations to make collecting data more efficient. Sherrell and Ariel were gold at illustrating, so they did all the beautiful drawings of each of the constellations while Livia worked on the colour theory and assembling all the constellations as geographically accurate as the A4 paper would allow. In the future, we might explore having more colours than just the three, or going bigger than A4 so we could make the map more accurate to the actual sky.
